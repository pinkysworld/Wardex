How BSL 1.1 works in practice

Release version: v0.52.5

Change Date: The BSL 1.1 licence converts to Apache 2.0 on 1 April 2029. After that date, all usage — including commercial — is permitted under Apache 2.0 terms.

Production use: Any commercial or production environment requires a separate commercial licence. Non-production use — development, research, internal evaluation — is fully permitted under BSL 1.1 itself.

Verification: Release archives and container images ship with SLSA provenance attestations, CycloneDX SBOMs, and cosign signatures — verification steps are published in Reproducibility & Supply-Chain Verification.

Questions? For licensing, commercial use, or partnership discussions, please contact us directly.

Licensing FAQ

Answers to the common questions.

Can I use Wardex at work for internal evaluation?

Yes. Internal evaluation, proof-of-concept, and development environments are covered under the BSL 1.1 free tier. Production or customer-facing deployments require a commercial licence.

What exactly counts as "production" use?

Any environment where Wardex is monitoring or protecting real business operations, customer data, or live workloads. If your SOC depends on it to detect real attacks, that's production.

Is Wardex truly open-source?

The source code is fully open, readable, forkable, and modifiable. BSL 1.1 is a source-available licence with a time-locked conversion to Apache 2.0. It's not OSI-certified open-source during the BSL window, but it's functionally open for every use case except production commercial deployment.

Can I contribute to Wardex?

Absolutely. Contributions are welcome — see CONTRIBUTING.md for the workflow. Contributors retain copyright of their contributions under a standard CLA.

What does a commercial licence include?

Production rights, priority support, direct engineering access, and deployment assistance. Exact scope is tailored to your deployment shape — contact us for a quote.

What happens after the Change Date?

On 1 April 2029, every Wardex release up to that point converts automatically to Apache 2.0. No restrictions, no separate licence needed — the full product becomes traditional open-source.
